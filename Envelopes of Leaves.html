Envelopes of Leaves

Created:1/25/2018 11:12 AM
Updated:1/25/2018 1:21 PM
Author:Lisa Mitchell Parker

Strapped into the grey passenger’s seat 
as we drive through falling, rising roads up north.  
Reds and yellows flame the hillsides around me 
a few leaves every mile rest on the windshield
before shooting off again.  
Every autumn I used to corral those brilliant colors,
and trap them inside a decorated envelope
to mail to my grandpa in Florida,
where palm trees stay the same and chlorophyll never dies.  
We learned in biology that those beautiful colors are in the leaves all year, 
but the green covers everything until it finally fades out in fall.  
I didn’t know that then, 
when I’d carefully collect the brightest leaves to send to Papa.  
I thought that the trees decided to look glamorous 
just before the snow came 
splatter-painting the ground, buildings and passing cars
so everything would be noticed.

Once when we were five, 
Peter Tomich made a whole pile of yellow leaves
he had yanked off a short tree branch before it was time for them to fall, 
and set them on fire with a magnifying glass.  
The yellow crinkled up in the spot of magnified sun and turned a dirty brown.  
Each leaf it touched caught the brown until the whole pile looked dead.  
I let out a huge scream of laughter and joy as the leaves lit up again in a burst of orange flame.  
Peter cried as the flames slapped his skin.  
He ran home.  
The flames were sucked into the ground 
until all that was left in that field 
was a pile of smoking ash.

One September, 
before I was nine, 
Papa died 
before the leaves were ready
to be packed up
and sent to Florida.  
They told me that Papa was cremated.  
No one wanted to say what that meant when I asked.  
My cousin, Matt, finally pulled me aside 
and told me it meant Papa was burned up.  
All I could picture was that pile of ash 
left from the flaming yellow leaves Peter Tomich made— 
the grey smoking dust 
being blown around by the wind.
