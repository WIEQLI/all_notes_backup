I was born just outside Philly and my family had a place either in Manahawkin or on the Island since the 50s or the 60s, my grandparents lived here full-time in the 70s and my dad set up shop here full-time about 15 years ago. (All Purpose Repairs)   I'll get you the actual numbers but my earliest memories are of this island (and the extended prelude of oldies that, once in a while, or almost synchronized with the fwap fwapping of our tires across the joints on rt 72 back when it was still concrete) drenched in coppertone, cue clanging soundtrack courtesy of old bells and rigging or was it the ice cream man's holler and beckon from the top of the dune, his truck perched strategically where the ashpalt switched to sand so we could stand, in line, up the final hill from the real world, scene unfolding like a brochure, revealing the Atlantic like technicolor from b&w. like talkies from the silent pictures; only this time from narrow to wide, expanding sonically as much as visually, a decompression incrementally as the narrow lane widens out in sight and sound,
sun so bright and sand, hot, scorching big to pinky heel toe dancing
or shuffle down deep into cool sand
stand a bit and set up to sit down
crispy and thundering surf,
moistening the skin and senses
with the impossible infinite threat of endless sea,
taunting like God;
only the biggest thing
you've ever actually seen;
except maybe for the moon or sky
but, of course, this...
this you could touch. or
even try to traverse,
This challenge you couldn't dismiss as mythical, ethereal...  no, worse
it made me measure myself against it,
taunting:
“how far are you willing
to venture from land, little boy?"

I hadn't yet learned how to tell something that big to go to hell.
but i would learn.
I'm surely an expert now.
how many nights have we writhed until dawn, telling God himself to get on with it or to join us in a swan dive into the abyss.
